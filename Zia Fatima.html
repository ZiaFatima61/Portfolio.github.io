Shopping Mall Management

The management of a shopping mall can be broken down into different parts, including Store & Tenant Management, Visitor Navigation & Flow Management, and Mall Inventory & Product Search.

SDG Alignment

  • SDG 8 – Decent Work & Economic Growth
  • SDG 9 – Industry, Innovation & Infrastructure
  • SDG 11 – Sustainable Cities & Communities
  • SDG 12 – Responsible Consumption & Production

Store & Tenant Management

What is required

  • A digital Tenant Management System
  • Automated shop allocation system
  • Contract & rent tracking dashboard
  • Store Connectivity & Utility Planning

Implementation Steps

  • AVL Tree - to store tenant and shop details using the unique Shop ID as the key, enabling efficient lookup while maintaining sorted order.
  • Priority Queue (Max-Heap) - For automated shop allocation. Shops or tenants are prioritized based on factors like demand, rent range, footfall zone, or business category.best available shop first, making allocation fast, fair, and optimized.
  • Binary Search Tree - To manage shop contracts sorted by expiry dates. It helps the system quickly access the earliest expiring contract, insert new ones, or update the existing ones in O(log n) time. This ensures smooth contract renewals and timely notifications.
  • Linked Lists - To maintain shop lists. Shops on each floor or zone are stored as nodes in a linked list (shop ID, tenant, category, status). It allows the system to add new shops, remove tenants, or mark vacancies in O(1) time without shifting elements like in arrays. This ensures smooth handling of frequent shop changes, making vacancy updates fast and flexible.
  • Kruskal’s Algorithm - To find minimum cost network (wiring, security lines, Wi-Fi).
  • Red-Black Tree - To organize stores into floors and zones, allowing fast and efficient store lookup and management.

Visitor Navigation & Flow Management

What is required

  • A digital mall map shop locator
  • floor navigation system
  • Crowd-flow heatmap display

Implementation Steps

  • Graphs - To model the mall layout because each shop, corridor, floor, and escalator can be represented as nodes and edges. This allows us to simulate real-world movement inside the mall.This allows instant O(1) lookup, useful for tasks like: “Who is in shop S105?” or “Show details of shop S220.”
  • BFS - To provide the shortest walking path on the same floor. BFS ensures quick, unweighted pathfinding such as “Find Shop A → Shop B” within a floor.
  • Dijkstra’s Algorithm - For multi-floor navigation where edges (elevators, escalators) have different weights. This gives the most efficient route considering distance, waiting time, and convenience.
  • Trie - To locate shops and facilities based on name or prefix efficiently.
  • Max-Heap - To identify the most crowded zones in real-time. The zone with the highest crowd count always rises to the top, helping in crowd control planning.

Mall Inventory & Product Search

What to build

  • A product search system for mall-wide stores
  • Product availability dashboard
  • Smart restocking
  • Trending Product Detection System

Implementation Steps

  • Trie - To give instant auto-suggestions for product names. This helps users type "nic" and instantly get “Nike”, “Nicolas Perfume”, etc.
  • KMP Algorithm - Used to efficiently search exact product names in large catalogs.
  • Boyer–Moore Algorithm - used for fast keyword search in long product descriptions and reviews.
  • Lookup Table - allow instant access from a keyword to related products.
  • Binary Search - On sorted product lists to instantly check if a product exists in a specific shop. This gives O(log n) search time.
  • Merge Sort - To sort products by price, brand, or popularity. This creates smooth and efficient product filtering in the mall app.
  • Hash Maps - To store product availability for every store. Example: “Is this product available in Store S110?” → instant lookup.
  • Fenwick Tree - To track frequency of product purchases.
  • Heap Sort - To find top-k trending products.
  • Sparse Table - To quickly analyze product sales trends over different time ranges (daily/weekly)

Smart Forest Fire Detection System

A forest is divided into zones, and each zone has sensors.
We treat the forest as a graph and the fire behavior as a pathfinding and pattern detection problem.

SDG Alignment

  • SDG 11 – Sustainable Cities and Communities
  • SDG 13 – Climate Action
  • SDG 15 – Life on Land

Implementation Steps

  • The entire forest is represented as a graph (BFS/DFS) - Nodes as Sensor locations, Edges as Connectivity between sensor nodes, Weights as Distance, vegetation difficulty, or fire spread time
  • Priority Queue - Used to prioritize highest temperature nodes, areas with maximum smoke, fastest fire-spreading zones
  • Circular Queue - Used to store continuous sensor readings
  • Binary Search Tree - Used to maintain sorted order of risk levels
  • Dijkstra’s Algorithm - Used for finding shortest path for firefighters and nearest water source location
  • Minimum Spanning Tree (Prim's) - Used to connect sensors with minimum wiring cost, reduce communication links, Optimize maintenance routes

Impact :

Using data structures and algorithms—like sensor data trees, graph-based alert networks, and pattern-detection algorithms—the smart fire detection system identifies fires early and sends alerts quickly. This reduces response time, prevents large-scale forest damage, and protects wildlife, nearby communities, and natural resources. Overall, it creates a safer, more resilient, and environmentally sustainable forest ecosystem.

Tourism Management

It cover navigation, parking, inventory, local business promotions, personalised user experience, crowd management, safety and customer service.

SDG Alignment

  • SDG 8 – Decent Work & Economic Growth
  • SDG 9 – Industry, Innovation & Infrastructure
  • SDG 11 – Sustainable Cities & Communities
  • SDG 12 – Responsible Consumption & Production
  • SDG 17 – Partnerships for the Goals (Indirect)

System Requirements

  • Hotel & Resort Booking Optimization
  • Tourism Recommendation System
  • Transport & Route Optimization
  • Digital Ticketing & Pass Management
  • Environmental & Heritage Protection

Implementation Steps

  • AVL Tree -To sort price list We use an AVL Tree to maintain hotel and resort prices in sorted order, ensuring quick updates and efficient searching for budget-friendly options.
  • Hash Maps - To store booking details Customer bookings, room IDs, dates, and payment status are stored in a Hash Map for O(1) access during check-ins and cancellations.
  • Greedy Algorithm - To allocate best available room quickly The system picks the optimal available room (closest match to price, view, or preference) without checking all combinations, ensuring fast confirmation.
  • Backtracking - To handle overbooking scenarios When double bookings occur, backtracking helps explore alternate room combinations and rearrange assignments to avoid conflicts.
  • Quick Sort + Binary Search - To suggest activities in order Activities are sorted by time, popularity, or category. Binary search helps the system quickly suggest suitable activities based on user preferences.
  • ArrayList (linked list)- To store visit history A dynamic list stores each tourist’s visited places in sequence, allowing quick addition and easy retrieval for recommendations.
  • Floyd–Warshall- All pairs shortest paths for route optimization This algorithm computes the shortest travel route between every pair of tourist spots, helping in planning a complete day-wise itinerary.
  • Hash Map, Queue, Stack- To store pass info, ticket counters (queue), undo operations (stack) Hash Maps store pass details, Queues manage visitors waiting at ticket counters, and Stacks help undo booking or navigation actions.
  • Merge Sort - To arrange visitors by entry time Visitors entering at different times are sorted to ensure smooth crowd control and during peak-hour analytics.
  • DFS - To detect abnormal activities in forests DFS traverses forest surveillance data to identify unusual movements or missing sensor signals, supporting wildlife protection.
  • Pattern Recognition Rabin-Karp – To detect illegal activities from logs Rabin–Karp quickly searches large logs for suspicious keywords or repeated illegal patterns in travel records.

Smart Water And Electricity System

SDG Alignment

  • SDG 6 – Clean Water and Sanitation
  • SDG 7 – Affordable and Clean Energy
  • SDG 9 – Industry, Innovation & Infrastructure
  • SDG 11 – Sustainable Cities and Communities

Implementation Steps

Smart Water Management system :

  • Model water pipelines as a Directed Weighted Graph
  • Use Floyd–Warshall Algorithm to detect inefficient water routes and compute all-pair shortest flow paths
  • Apply Union–Find (Disjoint Set) to identify connected and disconnected water supply regions
  • Store water consumption logs in a Binary Search Tree (BST) for fast range queries

Smart Electricity Distribution System :

  • Represent electrical grid circuits as a Graph with weighted edges for voltage/resistance
  • Use Bellman–Ford Algorithm to detect power fluctuations or negative-cycle faults in the grid
  • Apply Segment Trees to manage and query electricity usage across different time intervals
  • Store outage reports and repair tasks in a Circular Queue for efficient rotation and handling

Impact :

The integrated system improves resource efficiency by reducing leakages, energy loss, and service interruptions. Real-time monitoring ensures reliable delivery, while optimized distribution lowers operational costs and supports sustainable city growth. Together, they make essential utilities smarter, cleaner, and more resilient.
